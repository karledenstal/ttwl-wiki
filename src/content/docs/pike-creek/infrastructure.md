Pike Creek maintains the infrastructure needed to function independently for extended periods of time, particularly during harsh winters. While connected to regional systems, the town relies heavily on local resources and built-in redundancy to keep daily life running.

---
## Electricity & Power

Like most small towns, Pike Creek is connected to the regional power grid, with main lines running in along the primary road. While generally sufficient, the town is prone to outages and occasional brownouts during severe winter weather and storms.  
  
All critical buildings maintain backup gas or diesel generators, and many private businesses and households keep their own generators as well. Solar panels are increasingly common on both public and private buildings, providing supplemental power and reducing strain during peak demand.  
  
Power infrastructure in Pike Creek is largely managed by von Brandt Energy & Infrastructure. The company’s name appears on many local utility bills, and its influence is felt throughout the town. Over the years, von Brandt-backed initiatives have focused on modernizing infrastructure, promoting local renewable energy sources, and reducing reliance on coal, while working closely with the regional grid to maintain stability.  
  
In addition to solar energy, Pike Creek makes limited use of hydroelectric generation. Rather than large-scale projects such as dams, this takes the form of repurposed old mill infrastructure and low-head turbines installed along river outflows from Hemlock Lake. These systems provide modest but reliable supplemental power, particularly during peak seasons.  
  
Most buildings in Pike Creek rely primarily on electric heating, though natural gas lines remain in use in parts of town. Propane tanks are common for homes outside the main districts, and wood-burning stoves and fireplaces are widespread throughout Pike Creek. During harsher winters, many households rely on multiple heating sources rather than a single system.

## Water, Waste & Sewage

Water is drawn from Hemlock Lake and treated locally. Pipes tend to be older in Old Town, while newer and more actively maintained systems are found in Market and Harbor Row. The water is safe to drink, though it can occasionally have a faint metallic taste.  
  
Municipal sewage serves Market and much of Harbor Row, while older, partially updated systems remain in Old Town. Septic tanks are common for homes and cabins located outside the town proper.  
  
Trash collection takes place twice a week. Recycling is encouraged, with at least one small recycling center located in each district, though participation is not enforced. Dumps and transfer stations are located outside of town.

## Communications

The Byrd Broadcast Tower handles much of Pike Creek’s communications infrastructure. It maintains a reliable local radio broadcast and monitors weather conditions in the area.  
  
The local radio station is WBPC 106.1, Pike Creek Public Radio. While it’s possible to tune into other stations, reception is often unstable and accompanied by static. WBPC runs standard programming during the day, but shifts to its nighttime block, commonly referred to as 106.1 Echo FM. Echo FM is usually automated, though it is hosted live two nights a week by _Ruth Wolfe_.  
  
Internet access is available throughout Pike Creek, though speeds vary by location. Cell service is generally decent, but can be spotty, particularly near the forest and lake.

## Healthcare & emergencies

Pike Creek is served by a small number of medical clinics that handle routine care and minor emergencies. Ambulances transport patients to a larger nearby town, while more serious cases may be airlifted if weather conditions allow.  
  
The Byrd family owns and operates a helicopter, which is occasionally used in emergency situations due to their familiarity with the area and its terrain.  
  
The fire department consists of volunteer and semi-professional firefighters. Law enforcement is limited in size and led by a Chief of Police.
